ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE IN FINANCIAL SERVICES — INDUSTRY OVERVIEW 2025

INTRODUCTION

Artificial intelligence is transforming the financial services industry at an
unprecedented pace. Banks, insurers, and asset managers are deploying AI across
their operations — from customer service and fraud detection to risk management
and regulatory compliance. The technology has moved from experimental pilots to
production systems handling millions of transactions daily.

CURRENT STATE OF AI ADOPTION

85% of financial services firms are currently using some form of AI in their
operations. The most common applications are fraud detection, credit scoring,
customer service automation, and anti-money laundering. Machine learning models
now process loan applications in seconds that previously took days of manual
review.

Generative AI has created a significant step change since 2023. Large language
models are being used to summarise documents, draft communications, answer
customer queries, and assist analysts with research. Investment banks are using
AI to analyse earnings calls, news sentiment, and market data simultaneously.

RETRIEVAL AUGMENTED GENERATION IN FINANCE

RAG systems are particularly valuable in financial services because they allow
AI to answer questions using verified, up to date documents rather than relying
on training data that may be outdated or incorrect. A compliance team can ask
questions about regulatory documents and receive accurate answers with source
citations. A relationship manager can query a client's full history and get
instant summaries. Risk teams can interrogate thousands of pages of policy
documents in seconds.

The key advantage of RAG over fine-tuning in financial services is auditability.
Because answers are grounded in specific retrieved documents, compliance teams
can verify exactly where an answer came from. This is critical in regulated
environments where decisions must be explainable and traceable.

AGENTIC AI

Agentic AI refers to AI systems that can plan, reason, and take sequences of
actions autonomously to complete complex tasks. Unlike a simple chatbot that
responds to one question at a time, an agent can break a goal into steps, use
tools like web search or databases, check its own work, and iterate until the
task is complete.

In financial services, agentic AI is being applied to automate complex workflows
such as loan processing, claims handling, regulatory reporting, and portfolio
rebalancing. An agent can read a document, extract key information, cross
reference it against a database, flag anomalies, and produce a summary report —
all without human intervention.

FRAUD DETECTION AND RISK MANAGEMENT

AI has significantly improved fraud detection in banking. Traditional rule-based
systems flagged transactions based on fixed thresholds. Machine learning models
can identify complex patterns across millions of transactions in real time,
catching sophisticated fraud that rule-based systems miss while reducing false
positives that frustrate genuine customers.

Credit risk models now incorporate alternative data sources — utility payments,
rental history, mobile phone usage — alongside traditional credit scores to
assess borrowers more accurately. This has expanded access to credit for
customers who were previously excluded by traditional scoring methods.

REGULATORY COMPLIANCE AND RESPONSIBLE AI

Financial services is one of the most heavily regulated industries in the world.
AI systems must be explainable, fair, and auditable. Regulators require firms to
demonstrate that their AI models do not discriminate unfairly and that decisions
can be explained to customers.

The EU AI Act, which came into effect in 2024, classifies most financial services
AI as high risk, requiring mandatory human oversight, detailed documentation, and
regular testing for bias. Firms must maintain an inventory of all AI systems and
assess their compliance regularly.

Responsible AI means building systems that are transparent, fair, reliable, and
safe. It is not just about compliance — organisations that build trustworthy AI
systems see better business outcomes because customers and regulators trust them.

LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS IN FINANCIAL SERVICES

Large language models are being used across financial services for document
analysis, report generation, customer communication, and knowledge management.
Banks are using LLMs to analyse earnings transcripts and regulatory filings.
Insurance companies are using them to process claims documents and customer
correspondence. Asset managers are using them to synthesise research reports.

The main risks of LLMs in finance are hallucination — where the model generates
plausible but incorrect information — and the use of outdated training data.
RAG systems address both risks by grounding model outputs in verified, current
documents.

EMBEDDINGS AND SEMANTIC SEARCH

Embeddings are numerical representations of text that capture meaning. Similar
concepts have similar embeddings even if they use different words. This enables
semantic search — finding relevant information based on meaning rather than
keyword matching.

In financial services, semantic search allows analysts to find relevant
precedents, regulations, and case studies using natural language queries rather
than exact keyword searches. A query for credit risk policy will return relevant
documents even if they use terms like lending guidelines or borrower assessment.

Vector databases such as ChromaDB and Pinecone store these embeddings and enable
fast similarity search across millions of documents. This is the foundation of
RAG systems in financial services.

CHALLENGES AND FUTURE OUTLOOK

The main challenges facing AI adoption in financial services are data quality,
talent shortages, regulatory uncertainty, and third party risk. 83% of firms say
their AI adoption would be faster if they had stronger data infrastructure.

Looking ahead, agentic AI and multimodal models — which can process text,
images, audio, and structured data together — are expected to create the next
wave of transformation. The firms that invest in AI engineering talent and
infrastructure today will have a significant competitive advantage in the years
ahead.
